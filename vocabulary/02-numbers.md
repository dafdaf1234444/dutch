# Numbers

Numbers are essential for shopping, telling time, giving your phone number, and understanding prices. Dutch numbers follow a pattern similar to German: compound numbers are built "ones-and-tens" (e.g., 21 = *eenentwintig*, literally "one-and-twenty"). Once you learn the building blocks, you can construct any number.

## Compound Numbers (21–99)

Dutch compound numbers place the **ones digit first**, connected by **en** (and), followed by the tens digit. This is the opposite of English:

| Number | Dutch | Literal Meaning | Pronunciation Hint |
|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | eenentwintig | one-and-twenty | AYN-en-TVIN-tikh |
| 22 | tweeëntwintig | two-and-twenty | TVAY-en-TVIN-tikh |
| 33 | drieëndertig | three-and-thirty | DREE-en-DAYR-tikh |
| 44 | vierenveertig | four-and-forty | FEER-en-FAYR-tikh |
| 55 | vijfenvijftig | five-and-fifty | VAYF-en-VAYF-tikh |
| 67 | zevenenzestig | seven-and-sixty | ZAY-vun-en-ZES-tikh |
| 78 | achtenzeventig | eight-and-seventy | AHKH-ten-ZAY-vun-tikh |
| 85 | vijfentachtig | five-and-eighty | VAYF-en-TAKH-tikh |
| 99 | negenennegentig | nine-and-ninety | NAY-khun-en-NAY-khun-tikh |

**Pattern:** `[ones] + en + [tens]`

> Note the **trema** (ë) that appears when *en* follows a vowel, e.g., **tweeëntwintig**, **drieëndertig**. This signals that the *e* in *en* is a separate syllable.

## Ordinal Numbers (1st–10th)

Ordinals are formed by adding **-de** or **-ste** to the cardinal number. The first three are irregular.

| Dutch | English | Pronunciation Hint | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| eerste | first (1st) | AYR-stuh | Dit is mijn eerste bezoek aan Nederland. |
| tweede | second (2nd) | TVAY-duh | Neem de tweede straat links. |
| derde | third (3rd) | DAYR-duh | Het is de derde deur rechts. |
| vierde | fourth (4th) | FEER-duh | Zij woont op de vierde verdieping. |
| vijfde | fifth (5th) | VAYF-duh | De vijfde maand is mei. |
| zesde | sixth (6th) | ZES-duh | Ik ben de zesde in de rij. |
| zevende | seventh (7th) | ZAY-vun-duh | De zevende dag is zondag. |
| achtste | eighth (8th) | AHKHT-stuh | Mijn verjaardag is op de achtste. |
| negende | ninth (9th) | NAY-khun-duh | September is de negende maand. |
| tiende | tenth (10th) | TEEN-duh | Dit is de tiende keer! |

> **Rule of thumb:** 1st–19th generally take **-de** (except *achtste*). From 20th onward, ordinals take **-ste** (twintigste, dertigste, etc.).

## Phone Number Conventions

In the Netherlands, phone numbers are typically read out **digit by digit**. Mobile numbers start with **06** and are often grouped as:

**06 – 1234 5678** (read as: *nul zes – een twee drie vier, vijf zes zeven acht*)

For landlines, the area code comes first:
**020 – 123 4567** (Amsterdam) — read as: *nul twintig – een twee drie, vier vijf zes zeven*

> **Tip:** The number **2** is sometimes said as *twee* in phone contexts to avoid confusion. Dutch speakers say each digit clearly and separately.

## Mini Dialogue

> **Verkoper:** Goedemiddag! Kan ik u helpen?
>
> **Klant:** Ja, hoeveel kost dit T-shirt?
>
> **Verkoper:** Dat kost vijfentwintig euro.
>
> **Klant:** Vijfentwintig? Dat is een beetje duur. En die daar?
>
> **Verkoper:** Die kost vijftien euro. De tweede is goedkoper.
>
> **Klant:** Prima, ik neem die voor vijftien euro. Alstublieft, hier is twintig euro.
>
> **Verkoper:** Dank u wel. Vijf euro wisselgeld terug. Nog een prettige dag!
>
> **Klant:** Bedankt, tot ziens!
