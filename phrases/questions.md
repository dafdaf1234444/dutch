# Asking Questions in Dutch

## 1. Question Words

| **Dutch** | **English** | **Pronunciation** | **Example Question** | **Example Answer** |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Wie? | Who? | /wi/ | Wie is dat? | Dat is mijn broer. |
| Wat? | What? | /wɑt/ | Wat is dit? | Dit is een boek. |
| Waar? | Where? | /waːr/ | Waar is het station? | Het station is daar. |
| Wanneer? | When? | /wɑˈneːr/ | Wanneer vertrek je? | Ik vertrek morgen. |
| Waarom? | Why? | /waːrˈɔm/ | Waarom leer je Nederlands? | Omdat ik in Nederland woon. |
| Hoe? | How? | /hu/ | Hoe gaat het? | Het gaat goed. |
| Welk/Welke? | Which? | /wɛlk/ /ˈwɛlkə/ | Welke trein? | De trein naar Amsterdam. |
| Hoeveel? | How much/many? | /huˈveːl/ | Hoeveel kost dit? | Dit kost vijf euro. |
| Hoe lang? | How long? | /hu lɑŋ/ | Hoe lang duurt het? | Het duurt een uur. |
| Hoe laat? | What time? | /hu laːt/ | Hoe laat is het? | Het is drie uur. |

## 2. Forming Yes/No Questions

In Dutch, you form yes/no questions by **inverting the verb and the subject**. The conjugated verb moves to the front of the sentence:

| **Statement** | **Question** |
|---------------|-------------|
| Jij spreekt Nederlands. | Spreek jij Nederlands? |
| Hij woont in Amsterdam. | Woont hij in Amsterdam? |
| Zij heeft een hond. | Heeft zij een hond? |
| Jullie komen morgen. | Komen jullie morgen? |

> **Note:** When *jij/je* is inverted (placed after the verb), the verb **drops the -t** ending. This is a key rule in Dutch:
>
> - Jij **spreekt** Nederlands. → **Spreek** jij Nederlands?
> - Jij **werkt** vandaag. → **Werk** jij vandaag?
> - Jij **hebt** tijd. → **Heb** jij tijd?
>
> This *-t* drop only applies to *jij/je*, not to *hij/zij/het/u*.

## 3. Question Tags

Dutch uses several question tags to turn statements into confirmation questions:

| **Tag** | **Meaning** | **Example** |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| toch? | right? / isn't it? | Je komt morgen, toch? *(You're coming tomorrow, right?)* |
| hè? | isn't it? / right? | Het is mooi weer, hè? *(Nice weather, isn't it?)* |
| of niet? | or not? | Dat is goed, of niet? *(That's good, or not?)* |

These tags are added to the end of a statement and are very common in everyday Dutch conversation.

## 4. Common Question Patterns

Here are 15 useful question patterns you can adapt for many situations:

| **#** | **Dutch** | **English** | **Usage** |
|-------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| 1 | Kunt u mij helpen? | Can you help me? | Politely asking for help (formal) |
| 2 | Mag ik iets vragen? | May I ask something? | Politely starting a question |
| 3 | Weet u waar ... is? | Do you know where ... is? | Asking for directions (formal) |
| 4 | Heeft u ...? | Do you have ...? | Asking about availability (formal) |
| 5 | Spreekt u Engels? | Do you speak English? | Checking language ability (formal) |
| 6 | Hoe zeg je ... in het Nederlands? | How do you say ... in Dutch? | Learning vocabulary |
| 7 | Kunt u dat herhalen? | Can you repeat that? | Asking someone to repeat (formal) |
| 8 | Wat betekent ...? | What does ... mean? | Asking for a definition |
| 9 | Is er een ... in de buurt? | Is there a ... nearby? | Finding places or things nearby |
| 10 | Mag ik ...? | May I ...? | Asking permission |
| 11 | Zullen we ...? | Shall we ...? | Making suggestions |
| 12 | Heb je zin in ...? | Do you feel like ...? | Asking about interest or desire |
| 13 | Wat vind je van ...? | What do you think of ...? | Asking for an opinion |
| 14 | Ken je ...? | Do you know [a person]...? | Asking about familiarity with someone |
| 15 | Weet je ...? | Do you know [a fact]...? | Asking about knowledge of something |

> **Tip:** *Ken je* is used for knowing **people and places** (familiarity), while *weet je* is used for knowing **facts and information**. This distinction is important in Dutch!
